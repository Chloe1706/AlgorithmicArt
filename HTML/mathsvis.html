M.C. Escher Circle Limit

M.C. Escher in his studio in the 1971 film 'Adventures in Perception'

I have already discussed Escher‚Äôs work within this project however it felt relevant to mention his tessellation artworks again in context with the maths visualisation assignment. In particular, works from his ‚ÄúCircle Limit‚Äù series which follows rules of hyperbolic geometry; (a system that provides a mathematical framework for distortion and scaling where the pattern shrinks towards the edges). Additionally, these works also utilise tessellation; an intrinsically mathematical process which connects geometry and symmetry to create tiles that can fit together seamlessly over an infinite plane.

When tasked with researching artists and artworks that visualise mathematics, I first thought of Escher. What makes his tessellations so memorable is they depict recognisable imagery. Escher's process was one of intuition and experimentation. He would add a recognisable human dimension to an otherwise simple and stark mathematics; visualising mammals, birds and sea creatures within the tiles. This approach represents the perfect amalgamation of mathematics and visual art.

Hamid Naderi Yeganeh Mathematical Artwork

A Bird in Flight, by Hamid Naderi Yeganeh, 2016

Hamid Naderi Yeganeh is a mathematical artist whose work closely aligns with p5.js. His practice broadly utilises mathematical expressions such as trigonometric functions (sine, cosine etc), fibonacci sequences, exponential functions and the saw tooth wave as its primary medium. He writes programmes that evaluate these expressions and generate images.

Like Escher, Naderi Yeganeh will search for meaningful imagery or motifs within the generative art and submit these iterations for his artistic practice. An example of these can be seen here in the artwork titled ‚ÄúHeart‚Äù. In this piece, the heart motif is rendered using hundreds of line segments whose positions and orientations are determined by trigonometric equations, (equations that use functions like sin, cos and tan to express an angle as numbers that can be used to plot lines of shapes). This and many other works by Naderi Yeganeh adopt an iterative approach where something is generated, and then the equations are tweaked until the work resembles something. I would argue that works such as these are the contemporary answer to the blend of art and mathematics that was first introduced by individuals like Escher.

Interactivity

I added the mouseWheel function that behaves like an event listener with p5.js and responds when the user interacts with a scroll on a mouse or touchpad. This interaction is what increases the value of count and so as the user scrolls, the loop repeats and the shape shifts, forming this complex spiral.

With the guidance of Diana, I made some minor adjustments to improve the scroll consistency of the mouse as I found that to begin with, when scrolling with the mouse wheel the image tended to jut forward. To resolve this, I implemented an if statement where if the value assigned to the scroll is more than five, it is reverted to five. This results in a smoother effect.
