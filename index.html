Introduction

I thought I would begin this documentation by first defining what a grid can be in conjunction with art. The research on this topic is grounded in the theoretical framework established in the 1979 essay “Grids,” in which art historian and critic Rosalind Krauss critically explores the role of the grid in Modernist art.

Krauss’s essay; ‘Grids’ alludes to the The grid as a symbol of modernity through its rejection of nature and emphasis on aesthetic order over imitation. This, broadly speaking, aligned with the sentiments held by numerous core modernist art movements, for instance; Cubism, Constructivism, De Stijl and many more.

An alternative interpretation of a grid is a system in which the position of visual components are defined relative to one another, with the integration and repetition of a subject, flat colours, lines or shapes. To reiterate Krauss’s argument, grids of varying definitions play a significant role within modernist art as they contribute to the unifying goal of visual equilibrium and balance, in rejection of pure realism; held by movements that fall within Modernism.

All this to say that the term ‘grid’ can be flexible when used in context with art, however we can be certain that fundamentally it supports an aesthetic grounded in form and balance.

Pablo Picasso Latent Structural Grid

Pablo Picasso in his studio at Vallauris, 1953 · Photo © Cahiers d'Art

Take Cubism for instance, where the grid rarely appears as a regular lattice; instead it operates as an armature, underpinning the composition by organising fragmentation and assisting with relational positioning.

Ma Jolie, painted by Pablo Picasso between 1911 and 1912 during the height of Analytical Cubism, exemplifies the idea of the grid as a structural logic that underpins the composition. Its purpose is to sustain the fragmentation of the work while also providing some structural stability that evokes a feeling of balance in an otherwise complex, non-hierarchal composition. Picasso’s work anticipated what Krauss later identifies as the Modernist grid; a structural logic that contributes to a visual equilibrium that, oftentimes may be more conceptual than visual in nature.

Of course, there are more explicit use cases of the grid within Modernist Art. There is an argument to be made that Cubism prepared the Modernist grid for later, more literal interpretations.

This leads me into the art movement De Stijl and one of its pivotal central figures; Piet Mondrian:

Piet Mondrian Metaphysical Grid

Piet Mondrian in his studio, 1934 © Estate of Albert Gallatin · Photo © Albert Eugene

De Stijl was a Dutch art and design movement founded in 1917 which subscribed to a school of thinking by which artists and makers aimed to emphasise harmony and universality of colours and shapes. Principly, it focused on simplifying art to its most basic elements; primarily featuring geometric shapes and lines, and primary colours. This abstraction by means of reduction and simplification highlighted the relationships between form and colour.

Piet Mondrian was one of the central figures of the De Stijl movement, through which he expanded on his manifesto: ‘Neo-plasticism’; a theoretical framework which rejects representation in art, in favour of simplistic colors and lines to express visual harmony. This ideology was echoed in Rosaling Krauss’s 1979 essay: ‘Grids’. Without tangenting too much, it will become important to clarify at this stage that Mondrian’s grid was rooted in a metaphysical principle informed by Theosophy, (particularly its doctrines surrounding ideas of pure harmony and search of spiritual evolution). Mondrian's grid was a means to physically reconcile these ideas through his art.

In relation to the grid, Mondrian externalised what Cubism kept latent and the grid became the subject, not just the support. Crucially, the grid's purpose is to maintain visual equilibrium however, the inclusion of thick black vertical and horizontal lines highlighted its integrity to the composition. Many works and movements to arise from modernism emphasise the idea of flatness e.g. non-hierarchal composition, which the grid facilitates. However a key distinction between Mondrian and Cubists such as Picasso is that in Cubist work, flatness is achieved through fragmentation whereas, Mondrian and other artists of the De Stijl movement achieved flatness through clarity and reduction.

Conceptually, many of the core ideas behind algorithmic and generative art echo what Mondrian was doing by hand. Both share an emphasis on a parameter-based process of creation, where certain rules are laid out, for instance a limited colour palette and use of only orthogonal lines. This functions much like an algorithm where the outcome may vary slightly, however it will always abide by these predetermined parameters. Mondrian's commitment to visual equilibrium by means of his own strict rules resulted in a repetition of similar compositions. This parallels how a generative system can produce infinite results from following a similar set of parameters.

Jumping forward, an artist that utilises grids by Krauss’s loose definition but in a manner that is altogether more playful than austere, M.C. Escher:

M.C. Escher Procedural Grid

M.C. Escher, 1971 · Photo © Dutch National Archives

Maurits Cornelis Escher; or M.C Escher as he’s widely publicly known, was a Dutch artist whose work explored illusion, mathematics and perspective. Characterised as not belonging to a specific art movement at its time, Escher’s work was embraced later by mathematicians, scientists and the general public for bridging art and logic and is credited for its influence over everything from architecture to pop culture.

How grids manifest in Escher's work challenges a lot of the earlier notions where instead of drawn, the structure is inferred through repetition of interlocking units. Significantly, we can see here an instance where representation returns and abstract forms are replaced by recognisable motifs. The implication of this is that the composition is no longer stripped of narrative or meaning in place of aesthetic balance; but rather, narrative and representation, and balance and flatness exist in synchrony.

Specifically, Escher’s tesselations test the grids capacity to accommodate recognisable forms whilst still preserving a visual equilibrium and characteristic flatness. It is able to achieve this in examples like the one attached here because each figure functions as equal molecules with no foreground/background hierarchy and repetition and symmetry serve to flatten and balance the visual field. It is also facilitated by the mathematical precision with which Escher is associated.

A key differentiating factor between Mondrian and Escher and their individual purpose of the grid is that while Mondrian's motivations were rooted in spiritual grounding, Escher’s motivations were procedural and rooted in logic and mathematics. Escher is generally excluded from Modernist canon despite his technical sophistication. This is in part due to the fact he reintroduces subject depiction and violates certain Modernist taboos, placing him adjacent to Modernism as opposed to within it.

However, not too dissimilarly from Mondrian, Escher’s tessellation works could be described as algorithmic in a non-digital sense. If we were to define an algorithm in analog art as, a finite set of rules or parameters that, when repeatedly applied, generates a structured outcome; Escher’s polygonal tessellations follow a contingent logic that by its nature, could be likened to algorithmic logic. Crucially, for this reason, Mondrian and Escher were both intriguing case studies for exploring the intersection of grids and algorithms within traditional media.

However, This leads me onto to discuss my final case study in which the grid is elevated to a narrative device: Yayoi Kusama.

Yayoi Kusama Psychic Grid

Yayoi Kusama, Body-painting for Kusama's Self-Obliteration, 1960 · Photo © Fred Mcdarrah

Contrary to other artists mentioned, Kusama’s flatness, which is facilitated by grids, is used as a coping device. In order to understand the intent behind gridded works such as her Infinity Nets, we first have to dissect the narrative and psychological framework behind them. Her work is not symbolic, but rather a condition and manifestation of her self-described hallucinations in which she sees fields of multiplying patterns. Kusama herself describes this experience as ‘self-obliterating’.

Through mark making, Kusama’s grid emerges organically as she externalises her internal experience. If Picasso’s grid stabilises composition, Mondrian’s grid is tied to the universal order; with respects to his theosophic beliefs. And Escher’s grid is tied to procedural logic. Kusama’s grid is a reenactment of her condition, as the canvas becomes a record of lived psychic intensity. The result is a flat composition with no visual hierarchy which could be likened to the previous examples; however, the means by which the artist arrives at the outcome are entirely different.

Rosaling Krauss characterises the grid as a structure that enforced flatness, balance and neutrality. At first glance, Kusama’s Infinity Nets appear to conform to this logic however, upon closer examination, they reveal the grid’s paradoxical nature, maintaining formal flatness and balance while simultaneously they are imbued with subjectivity and psychic intensity; as Kusama presents the grid as a site where structural order and lived experience collide.

My hope is that through this research and case study analysis, I have presented that the grid is not a single form, but a shifting conceptual framework whose meaning depends on the context of its grounding. It simultaneously is and is not a neutral structure. When analysed in purely formal terms, it serves to flatten, balance and neutralise composition. However, it has the capacity to absorb ideology, metaphysics, mathematics and psychology depending on the artists who deploys it. This adaptability helps explain the grid's persistence in algorithmic and generative art where it reemerges as a procedural logic; most akin to Escher’s practice, through which form is produced. In such contexts, the grid operates as a rule based system capable of generating variation while still maintaining structural coherence under strict parameters, expanding on the grid's historical role within visual art.
